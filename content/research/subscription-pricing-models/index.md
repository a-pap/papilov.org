Since 2020, subscription prices have outpaced inflation by 3–5×. The US Consumer Price Index rose roughly 22% over this period. Most major subscriptions rose 50–170%.

Disney+ leads the pack with a 172% increase ($6.99 → $18.99), followed by Apple TV+ at 160%, Netflix at 125%, and Xbox Game Pass Ultimate doubling to $29.99. Meanwhile, World of Warcraft has charged exactly $14.99/month since November 2004 — 21 years with zero change.

This isn't random. It's three distinct pricing models operating on fundamentally different economic logic.

## Three Models

**Extractive pricing.** The subscription *is* the product. Netflix, Disney+, Spotify, HBO Max. Revenue comes directly from subscription fees, switching costs are moderate, and the strategy is to raise prices as fast as churn elasticity allows — typically 1.8–5.5% churn per dollar increase. Ad tiers serve as a pressure release valve, letting price-sensitive users downgrade instead of cancel.

**Gateway pricing.** The subscription is an acquisition tool. WoW, Costco, Planet Fitness, Amazon Prime. The sub itself operates near or below cost — the real money comes from adjacent spending. Costco's membership fee covers operating costs while product margins generate profit. Amazon Prime members spend roughly $1,400/year on Amazon versus $600 for non-members. WoW held its price at $14.99 while shifting from 85% subscription revenue (2004–2010) to roughly 50% subscription / 35% microtransactions and tokens / 15% expansions (2020–2026). They didn't leave money on the table — they moved the table.

**Monopoly pricing.** No real alternatives exist. Adobe Creative Cloud, Microsoft 365, Google Workspace. Extreme switching costs and proprietary format lock-in mean these companies can raise prices freely, citing AI features or platform improvements. Adobe and Microsoft have added 30–43% in recent years with minimal churn impact.

## Why We Keep Paying

Behavioral economics explains the inertia. Loss aversion means losing access to Netflix feels roughly twice as bad as the rational savings of canceling — 42% of users delay cancellation even when dissatisfied. 72% of consumers underestimate their total subscription spending by around 40%, and 55% maintain at least one subscription they don't actively use.

The most effective strategy is the boiling frog approach: small, regular increases of roughly 10% that stay below the psychological pain threshold. Netflix perfected this over 14 years — $1–2.50 every 12–24 months. The same total increase delivered as a single shock (Netflix's 2011 Qwikster disaster: 60% overnight, 800K subscribers lost, stock down 77%) triggers moral outrage. Consumers judge prices morally, not economically. 60% at once ≠ 5×12% spread over time.

## The Timeline

The history of subscription pricing is littered with both disasters and successful pivots. Netflix's 2011 Qwikster debacle, MoviePass's $9.95 death spiral (92.3% churn, executives convicted of fraud), EVE Online's 2022 backlash after breaking an 18-year price freeze — all cautionary tales. On the other side, Netflix's 2023–24 ad tier (94M users) and password crackdown (~50M new subscribers) showed how to grow revenue without proportional price pain.

The most recent major event: Xbox Game Pass Ultimate's 50% single hike in October 2025 ($19.99 → $29.99), which crashed their cancellation website and drew public mockery from GameStop. The lesson hasn't changed since 2011 — too much, too fast, no added value.
