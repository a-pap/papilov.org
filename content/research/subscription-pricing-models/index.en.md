Since 2020, subscription prices have outpaced inflation by 3–5×. The US Consumer Price Index rose roughly 22% over this period. Most major subscriptions rose 50–170%.

Disney+ leads the pack with a 172% increase ($6.99 → $18.99), followed by Apple TV+ at 160%, Netflix at 125%, and Xbox Game Pass Ultimate doubling to $29.99. Meanwhile, World of Warcraft has charged exactly $14.99/month since November 2004 — 21 years with zero change.

This isn't random. It's three distinct pricing models operating on fundamentally different economic logic.

$14.99 hasn't moved in 21 years. But Blizzard isn't leaving money on the table — they moved the table.

Bobby Kotick (ex-CEO): *"It's a prickly audience. You don't wanna do too much to agitate them. Even a dollar increase would've been a problem."*

**2004–2010:** Subscription was 85% of revenue. Expansions 15%. Microtransactions: zero.

**2020–2026:** Subscription dropped to ~50%. Microtransactions and tokens grew to ~35%. Expansions stayed at 15%.

They didn't raise the price. They built new revenue streams around a frozen anchor.

## Three Pricing Models

<div class="fw-grid">
  <div class="fw-card fw-card--red">
    <h4>Extractive</h4>
    <div class="fw-sub">Subscription = the product. Raise as fast as churn allows.</div>
    <div class="fw-tags"><span>Netflix</span><span>Disney+</span><span>Spotify</span><span>HBO Max</span></div>
    <ul class="fw-list">
      <li>Revenue comes from subscriptions</li>
      <li>Moderate switching costs</li>
      <li>Ad tiers as pressure release</li>
      <li>Churn elasticity ~1.8–5.5%</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="fw-card fw-card--green">
    <h4>Gateway</h4>
    <div class="fw-sub">Subscription = acquisition tool. Hold price, monetize elsewhere.</div>
    <div class="fw-tags"><span>WoW</span><span>Costco</span><span>Planet Fitness</span><span>Amazon Prime</span></div>
    <ul class="fw-list">
      <li>Sub drives adjacent spending</li>
      <li>Price = brand identity</li>
      <li>Raise once per decade (if ever)</li>
      <li>Prime members: $1,400/yr on Amazon</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="fw-card fw-card--purple">
    <h4>Monopoly</h4>
    <div class="fw-sub">No real alternatives. Raise freely, cite AI/features.</div>
    <div class="fw-tags"><span>Adobe CC</span><span>Microsoft 365</span><span>Google Workspace</span></div>
    <ul class="fw-list">
      <li>Extreme switching costs</li>
      <li>Proprietary format lock-in</li>
      <li>AI features justify +30–43%</li>
      <li>Minimal churn regardless</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

## Why We Keep Paying

<div class="psych-grid">
  <div class="psych-card">
    <h4>Loss Aversion</h4>
    <p>Losing Netflix feels 2× worse than the rational savings of canceling. 42% of users delay cancellation even when dissatisfied.</p>
    <div class="big">2×</div>
  </div>
  <div class="psych-card">
    <h4>Subscription Blindness</h4>
    <p>72% of consumers underestimate their total subscription spending by ~40%. 55% maintain at least one unused sub.</p>
    <div class="big">72%</div>
  </div>
  <div class="psych-card">
    <h4>Boiling Frog</h4>
    <p>Small ~10% increases stay below the pain threshold. Netflix: $1–2.50 every 12–24 months — too little to trigger action.</p>
    <div class="big">~10%</div>
  </div>
  <div class="psych-card">
    <h4>Moral Fairness</h4>
    <p>Consumers judge prices morally, not economically. Netflix's 2011 60% hike felt like betrayal. The same increase over 5 years felt normal.</p>
    <div class="big">60% ≠ 5×12%</div>
  </div>
